Trends in Prevalence of Doctor-diagnosed Arthritis：1999-2020



Arthritis, as a common health problem that affects millions of people worldwide, has become a significant hazard to people’s health well-being and daily activities, which causes stiffness and severe pain of one or more joints, leading to huge financial burden on both individuals and society. In order to shed a light on arthritis prevalence in general over the last 20 years, we conduct this serial cross-sectional study on the overall arthritis prevalence from 1999-2020 using multiple imputed NHANES data.

Trends in Prevalence of overall arthritis by different subgroups


Our results revealed that the overall estimated age-standardized prevalence of arthritis considerably increased from 27.19% (95% CI, 25.95%-28.42%) in 1999-2000 to 31.52% (95% CI, 30.64%-32.41%) in 2019-2020 (P for trend = .083).

This result may differ through different subgroups. For instance, over the past two decades, there was an increase by 23.3% in male and 11.1% in female. In some subgroups, including Mexican Americans, non-Hispanic-whites, and other races populations, people with a high school or incomplete college diploma, people with abdominal obesity, and in the subgroups of smokers and non-smokers, a more pronounced trend was found.

Comparison between RA and OA prevalence by different subgroups

Given the relatively low prevalence of psoriatic arthritis (PA) and other types of arthritis, our main focus centered on the prevalence of the two major subtypes, rheumatoid arthritis (RA) and osteoarthritis (OA). When examining trends from 1999-2000 to 2019-2020, the overall prevalence of RA in arthritis patients did not experience a significant change from 9.12% (95% CI, 8.23%-10.02%) to 6.96% (95% CI, 6.42%-7.51%) (P for trend = .43). However, the overall prevalence of OA increased from 8.23% (7.37%-9.08%) to 14.34% (13.61%-15.06%) (P for trend <.001). Remarkably, this trend was consistent across most subgroups, based on both genders, adults of all age groups, all races except non-Hispanic Asians, people with education level above high school, people of all BMI groups, people with or without abdominal obesity, people with or without insurance and people of all poverty index groups. Similar trends were observed in all smoking status groups, as well as in subgroups based on all levels of exercise. This strongly suggested the profound increase in OA prevalence from 1999 to 2020.
